What are eye diseases?

Eye diseases, or eye disorders, refer to a wide array of medical conditions that affect the eyes and can disrupt normal vision function. According to georgetown.org, approximately 8% of Americans suffer some sort of visual impairment, ranging from common and minor annoyances to severe and vision-threatening conditions. Eye disorders can manifest in numerous ways, with symptoms such as blurred vision, eye pain, redness, double vision, sensitivity to light, and changes in color perception. Left untreated, some eye disorders can progress and potentially result in vision loss or even blindness. Moreover, many eye disorders are chronic and require ongoing management to preserve visual function.

What are the most common disorders?

  • Refractive Errors: Myopia (nearsightedness), hyperopia (farsightedness), and astigmatism.
  • Cataracts: Clouding of the eye's lens, leading to blurry vision.
  • Glaucoma: Increased intraocular pressure, damaging the optic nerve.
  • Macular Degeneration: Gradual loss of central vision.
  • Diabetic Retinopathy: Damage to blood vessels in the retina due to diabetes.
  • Conjunctivitis (Pink Eye): Inflammation of the conjunctiva, causing redness and itching.
  • Dry Eye Syndrome: Insufficient production of tears or poor-quality tears.
  • Strabismus: Misalignment of the eyes, affecting coordination.
  • Amblyopia (Lazy Eye): Reduced vision in one eye due to abnormal visual development.
  • Retinal Detachment: Separation of the retina from its normal position.

Phoropter: an instrument comprised of cylinders, prisms, and lenses that measures the eye's refractive error to help determine an eye prescription
