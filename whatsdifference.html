While strabismus and amblyopia are often confused with one another, they are both very distinct and change different aspects of vision and eye alignment

Strabismus

Amblyopia

Strabismus, often referred to as "crossed eyes" or "squint," is a visual disorder characterized by an imbalance in the muscles that control eye movement. This imbalance causes one eye to deviate from its normal alignment, leading to misalignment of the eyes. Strabismus can be constant or intermittent and may manifest as one eye turning inward, outward, upward, or downward. This misalignment can result in double vision and impaired depth perception. It can be caused by various factors, including genetics, muscle weaknesses, or neurological issues (Cleveland Clinic).

Amblyopia, also known as "lazy eye," is a condition in which one eye does not develop normal vision during childhood. It often occurs as a consequence of strabismus, but it can also be caused by a difference in prescription between the eyes or other eye health issues (Boston Childrenâ€™s Hospital). In amblyopia, the brain tends to favor one eye over the other, leading to poor vision in the affected eye. If left untreated, amblyopia can result in permanent vision impairment in the weaker eye.

The relationship between strabismus and amblyopia lies in the fact that strabismus can lead to amblyopia (Optimax, 22). When the eyes are misaligned due to strabismus, the brain may suppress the input from one eye to avoid double vision. This suppression can cause amblyopia in the neglected eye. Conversely, amblyopia can contribute to the development of strabismus, as the misalignment of the eyes can worsen over time.

Muscles of the Eye

  • Superior Rectus Muscle: Aids in upward movement of the eye, especially when looking up or elevating the gaze.
  • Inferior Rectus Muscle: Primarily responsible for downward movement of the eye, assisting in looking downwards or depressing the gaze.
  • Medial Rectus Muscle: Allows for inward movement of the eye, facilitating convergence or bringing both eyes toward the midline.
  • Lateral Rectus Muscle Enables outward movement of the eye, responsible for divergence or moving the eyes away from the midline.
  • Superior Oblique Muscle: Helps in downward and outward movement of the eye, aiding in depression, abduction, and intorsion (rotating the top of the eye towards the nose).
  • Inferior Oblique Muscle: Supports upward and outward movement of the eye, assisting in elevation, abduction, and extorsion (rotating the top of the eye away from the nose).
  • Trochlea: A structure that acts as a pulley, providing a pivot point for the superior oblique muscle's action, allowing it to efficiently change the direction of its pull on the eyeball.
